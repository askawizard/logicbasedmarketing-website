How to Know When to Fire Your Marketing Agency (And What to Do Before You Hire the Next One)

📅 Feb 24, 2026 ⏱️ 15 min read ✍️ Tyler Sass

Most founders have fired at least 3 marketing agencies. And almost every time, they immediately hire a replacement doing the exact same thing. Just with a different name on the invoice. Here's how to break that cycle.

The Painful Truth About Agency Relationships

72%
Of marketers who say they cannot accurately measure ROI of marketing spend across all channels
Source: Forrester Research, 2023

Most founders have fired at least 3 marketing agencies. And almost every time, they immediately hired a replacement doing the exact same thing. Just with a different name on the invoice.

The pattern is so predictable it's almost a joke. You meet with Agency #4. They "get it" in the pitch room. They nod in all the right places. They talk about data-driven strategies and growth playbooks. Your founder brain thinks: Finally. This is the one.

Then Month 1 happens. They set everything up. Accounts are created. Campaigns launch. There's activity. There's energy. You see dashboards with charts pointing up.

Month 3 rolls around. The growth isn't what they promised. You ask about it. The answer is always a variation of the same story: the algorithm changed, seasonality hit different, attribution windows are wrong, the market is cooling.

By Month 5, you're frustrated. By Month 6, you're done. You fire them.

Then you hire Agency #5.

The reason this cycle repeats isn't always because the agencies are bad. Sometimes they are. But more often, it's this: you don't have a data layer underneath to know whether any agency is performing. You can't evaluate vendors without ground truth. And when you can't evaluate them, you can't trust them. So you fire them and try again.

This guide is about breaking that cycle. Not by learning how to find better agencies, but by learning how to see what's actually happening in your business so you can make a real decision about whether your current vendor is the problem, or whether the problem is something else entirely.

The Agency Relationship Cycle: Why It Always Follows the Same Path

Every founder reading this has lived this:

  • Week 1-2: Initial Excitement. The agency "gets it." They've done this before. They ask the right questions. You feel confident.
  • Week 3-8: First 60 Days of Activity. Campaigns launch. You see dashboards. Activity increases. Spend increases. Early "optimism" dashboards show everything looks good.
  • Month 3: "Performance Will Improve." Revenue isn't matching the activity. You raise it. They explain: it's early, attribution is still settling, you need to give it time, the conversion funnel has a lag.
  • Month 4-5: Growing Frustration. The revenue still isn't there. Now the agency is blaming: algorithm changes, platform changes, attribution windows, seasonality, market cooling, iOS 14.5, your landing page, your offer, your targeting parameters.
  • Month 6: Termination. You're done. You send the termination letter. You find a new agency.
  • Month 7: Rinse and Repeat. Agency #5 starts. Same pitch. Same energy. Same timeline. Same inevitable outcome.

The problem driving this cycle isn't the agencies alone. The problem is that agencies control the reporting. They show you their platform data. Google says the CAC is $45. Facebook says the ROAS is 2.1x. The dashboard is all green. But your bank account isn't moving.

You have a choice: trust the agency or fire them. There is no third option because you don't have independent visibility into what's actually happening.

Until you do.

The 7 Warning Signs Your Agency Is Underperforming

87%
Of marketing executives who say data quality issues directly impact their revenue decisions
Source: Salesforce State of Marketing, 2023

These aren't signs that your agency is bad. These are signs that you're missing data. And when you're missing data, you can't tell the difference between a bad agency and a data problem.

Warning Sign #1: They can only show you platform-reported data, never reconciled revenue

Your agency sends you a dashboard. Google Ads shows 500 conversions at a $50 CAC. Facebook shows 300 conversions at a $40 CAC. But when you look at your actual revenue in Shopify or Stripe, you only see 450 total new customers that month, and their average first purchase was $120, not $500.

Why it's a problem: Platform reporting is not reconciled to your actual business. Google counts a conversion as any form completion. You count it as a paying customer. These are different things. If your agency can't bridge that gap, they can't actually tell you if the campaign is working.

Warning Sign #2: Their reporting doesn't connect to your P&L

You ask: "What is the margin on the customers we acquired from Google this month?" They answer with questions: "Well, what are you counting as margin? Are you including fulfillment? Are you counting the customer service time?" Then they send you a report that shows ROAS and CAC but doesn't tell you if any of it is actually profitable at your cost structure.

Why it's a problem: Your P&L is the only number that matters. If the agency can't connect their work to your actual profit, they're flying blind. So are you.

Warning Sign #3: Your actual business revenue hasn't moved, but they show "positive trends"

Month 1 revenue: $145k. Month 2 revenue: $148k. Month 3 revenue: $143k. But the agency shows you a graph of "conversion volume increasing" or "cost per click decreasing" or "impression share growing." They talk about the positive trend in their metrics while your actual revenue is flat.

Why it's a problem: Vanity metrics are fine for diagnostics. They're terrible for decisions. If revenue isn't moving, nothing else matters. An agency that celebrates trend improvements while your business stays flat is optimizing the wrong thing.

Warning Sign #4: They blame seasonality or algorithm changes more than twice in 90 days

"The algorithm changed." "It's seasonal." "iOS 14.5 broke attribution." "Q4 is always harder." "Back-to-school season." These are all real things. But if your agency is citing them more than twice in a 90-day period, they're using them as excuses instead of adapting.

Why it's a problem: Real challenges are real. But real agencies adapt to real challenges instead of just explaining them away. If seasonality is a problem, the agency should be planning for it. If algorithm changes are a problem, the agency should be optimizing for it. If they're just explaining it, they're not actually solving it.

Warning Sign #5: When you ask for your raw data export, there's resistance or delay

You ask for the raw campaign data (every click, every conversion, every impression, every cost). The agency says: "We have everything in the dashboard." Or: "Let me check with our data team." Or: "That takes a while to pull." Or silence.

Why it's a problem: You own your data. Period. If the agency hesitates to give it to you, that's a red flag. What are they hiding? Nothing good. Good agencies hand over raw data without a conversation.

Warning Sign #6: You've asked "which campaigns are actually profitable" and never gotten a clear answer

You ask: "Of the campaigns you're running, which ones are cash-flow positive? Which ones are break-even? Which ones are losing money?" The answer is vague: "Overall the account is profitable." Or: "It depends how you allocate indirect costs." Or: "Give it more time to scale."

Why it's a problem: If the agency can't tell you which individual campaigns are profitable, they can't optimize to profitability. They're optimizing to whatever metric their platform shows them. That's not the same as optimizing your business.

Warning Sign #7: You have more channels running than you have clear attribution for

You're running Google, Facebook, TikTok, LinkedIn, email, and organic. Your agency is managing Google and Facebook. They're showing you ROAS for those channels. But you have 7 customer touch points across 6 channels before someone buys. Who gets credit? The agency credits Google. But maybe the customer found you on TikTok, clicked through to your email list, and bought from an email. Is that a Google ROI or a TikTok + Email ROI? You have no idea.

Why it's a problem: Attribution is hard. But if you don't have a clear attribution model, you're making decisions based on fiction. The agency is showing you how their channel performed in last-click attribution. That's one story. It might not be the true story of what drove the sale.

The Real Problem: Bad Agency vs. Bad Data Foundation

Here's the insight that changes everything: some agencies are genuinely bad. But many times, what looks like a bad agency is actually a data problem underneath.

And you can't tell the difference without independent data.

This is the reason the agency firing cycle repeats. You fire your agency because you can't see what they're actually delivering. Then you hire a new agency. And because nothing has changed in your data foundation, the new agency runs into the same problem. So you fire them too.

Let me give you a real example.

The Case of the "Bad" Agency That Was Actually a Data Problem

A B2C ecommerce brand is selling supplements. They've fired 4 agencies in 18 months. The pattern is always the same: initial growth, then plateau, then termination. They're frustrated. They think they're just unlucky with agencies.

They hire Agency #5. Month 2, they're already seeing the plateau. Agency #5 is showing Google generating profitable traffic. The ROAS is strong. The CPC is reasonable. Everything looks good on the platform.

But the founder is frustrated because total revenue hasn't grown.

Here's what we discovered: their Shopify-to-QuickBooks reconciliation was broken. Shopify was counting refunds as completed orders. So a customer who bought and refunded in the same month was being counted as a profitable customer in Shopify. But in QuickBooks (the actual financial record), it was a zero. The agency was actually generating profitable traffic. But the founder couldn't see it because their data didn't match their bank account.

The agency wasn't the problem. The data foundation was.

This happens more often than you'd think. The agency gets blamed for a problem they can't actually see. And the founder, not knowing the real issue, fires them and hires someone else to fail in the same exact way.

Your job before you fire anyone is to know the difference.

How to Audit Your Agency's Performance Yourself

You don't need permission from your agency. You don't need their help. You can do this yourself, right now, with data you own.

Step 1: Export Raw Data From the Agency's Platforms

Log into Google Ads. Log into Facebook. Export the raw campaign data for the last 90 days. This should include:

  • Every ad campaign and its spend
  • Every conversion from each campaign
  • Every click and impression
  • Cost per action (CPA), cost per click (CPC), cost per thousand impressions (CPM)
  • Conversion rate

You're not looking at summary dashboards. You're pulling raw numbers. If your agency is managing the account, you have access to this data. If you don't, that's your first red flag.

Step 2: Pull Your Actual Transaction Data

Go to your source of truth. For ecommerce, it's Shopify. For SaaS, it's Stripe or your payment processor. For lead gen, it's your CRM. Export the customer data for the same 90-day period:

  • Every customer who signed up or purchased
  • The date they converted
  • The amount they spent (or the value of the lead)
  • Any additional metadata: location, customer source (if available), customer type

This is the ground truth. This is what actually happened in your business. Not what the platform said happened. What actually happened.

Step 3: Try to Connect Them

Now the hard part. Take the Google data. A customer came in from Google on March 15th at 2:47pm. Cost: $12. Google says they converted (form submitted). Go to your Shopify data. Do you have a customer who purchased on March 15th or March 16th?

You're trying to draw a line from ad spend to actual revenue.

Most founders discover somewhere in this step that their systems don't actually talk to each other. You can match some customers. Not all. The timing doesn't always line up. The source attribution in Shopify doesn't always match what Google says. And that's where the audit usually stops.

That's where the real problem is.

Step 4: Calculate Your Real CAC

Not the platform number. Not what Google tells you. Your real CAC.

Real CAC = Total Spend ÷ Total New Customers

Total Spend includes:

  • All ad platform spend (Google, Facebook, etc.)
  • Your agency fee (if it's not included in the platform spend)
  • Ops overhead that touches customer acquisition (your salesperson's time if they're closing leads)
  • Fulfillment cost for the first order (if applicable)
  • Returns and refunds (subtract from revenue)

Total New Customers: Only count customers with a first purchase in the 90-day period. Don't count repeat purchases. Don't count customers who bought before. Count new customer acquisition only.

Let's say you spent $50,000 total (ads + agency fee + ops). You acquired 1,100 new customers. Your real CAC is $45.45.

Google might tell you your CAC is $35. But Google doesn't include your agency fee. Google doesn't include returns. Google doesn't include the fact that 20% of people who clicked your ad got lost in your funnel and never converted. Your real CAC is $45.45.

Step 5: Compare Real CAC to Real LTV

Now the final question: what's your real lifetime value (LTV)?

This is the total value a customer will generate for your business, accounting for repeat purchases, operating costs, and churn.

Real LTV = (Average First Purchase Value × Repeat Purchase Rate × Average Repeat Purchase Value × Customer Lifespan) - Operating Costs to Serve

Let's say your customers spend an average of $150 on their first purchase. 30% of them make a repeat purchase within 12 months, and those repeat purchases average $120. Your operating costs (shipping, customer service, payment processing) are about 35% of revenue.

Your real LTV is roughly: (150 × 1.3 × 120 × simplified) - 35% of revenue ≈ $235 per customer (simplified math).

If CAC ($45.45) < LTV ($235), the unit economics work. Your agency is generating profitable customers.

If CAC > LTV, you have a unit economics problem. It doesn't matter how good the agency is. You're losing money on each customer.

What to Do Before You Fire: The Pre-Termination Checklist

If you've done the audit and the math, you know whether the agency is the problem or the data foundation is. Before you pull the trigger on termination, go through this checklist.

Have You Given Them Access to Ground Truth Data?

Most agencies are working in the dark. You tell them: "We need to grow revenue." They start running ads. You share the platform dashboard with them. But you don't share your Shopify data. You don't share your CAC. You don't share your LTV. You don't share your profit margins.

You gave them "what performed" and not "what converted to revenue." You didn't give them what they need to optimize.

Before you fire them: give them access to your real numbers. Show them your actual CAC target. Show them your LTV. Show them your margin targets. Then give them 30 days to optimize to those numbers instead of the platform's numbers. You might be shocked what happens when an agency is actually optimizing for your business instead of Google's dashboard.

Have You Defined Success in Writing?

If you hired your agency and said "grow our business," that's not a definition of success. That's a direction. It's not measurable. It's not clear. Every agency will optimize to something, but if you didn't tell them what that something is, you're both guessing.

Before you fire them: write down what success looks like in numbers. "We want to acquire new customers at no more than a 3x CAC to LTV ratio." "We want monthly revenue growth of at least 8%." "We want to maintain a minimum ROAS of 2.5x after accounting for all costs." Specific. Measurable. Written down.

Then ask your agency: can you hit this? If they say yes, give them 60 days. If they say no, now you know the problem isn't the agency. It's that your numbers don't work. That's a different problem entirely.

Have You Done the Math Yourself?

Not their math. Your math. On your data. In a spreadsheet you control.

If you haven't done Step 1-5 of the audit, you don't have the right to fire your agency based on "not performing." You're firing them based on feelings, not math. And when you hire Agency #6, you'll be making the same decision with the same lack of data.

Do the audit first. Do the math. Then decide.

Is the Problem the Agency, or Is the Problem Your Funnel?

Sometimes the agency is bringing in traffic. They're doing their job. But the traffic doesn't convert because your landing page is broken. Or your offer is weak. Or your checkout flow has friction. Or your email sequence doesn't close.

You fire the agency. You hire a new one. The new one brings in the same traffic. It still doesn't convert. So you fire them too.

Before you fire anyone, ask: is this a traffic problem or a conversion problem? If traffic is coming in but not converting, the agency isn't the problem. Your funnel is.

Test this: take the traffic the agency is sending you. Run it through a different offer or landing page. Or run different messaging. If the same traffic converts better with different creative or offer, the agency needs to optimize. If the same traffic still doesn't convert, the problem isn't the agency.

How to Fire Cleanly: The Exit Conversation

If you've done the audit, done the math, given them access to ground truth data, defined success in writing, and the numbers still don't work, it's time to let them go. Do it cleanly.

Give 30-Day Written Notice

Not a surprise. Not a conversation that turns into an argument. Send a professional email stating that you're ending the relationship in 30 days. This gives you time to prepare for the transition and gives them time to wrap up work.

Request a Full Data Export

You want:

  • Raw campaign data (every click, impression, conversion for the entire relationship)
  • Audience data (the audiences they built, the sizes, the characteristics)
  • Creative assets (all ads, copy, landing pages)
  • Landing page access (if they built them, you need ownership and access)
  • Campaign documentation (what was the hypothesis, what did they test, what won)

You own this data. You're not asking for a favor. You're taking what's yours.

Get Passwords Transferred

Ad accounts, analytics, landing page builders, email platforms. Anything your agency has access to, you need access to. Get passwords transferred to you 5 days before the end of the relationship.

Don't Burn the Bridge

Sometimes you'll need them during transition. Maybe they continue month-to-month helping you move campaigns over. Maybe you realize 2 weeks after firing them that you need a specific report. Keep it professional. Keep it cordial.

Document What Worked

Before they leave, make sure you have documentation of what performed well. What audiences responded best? What creative? What offers? What messaging? You don't want to repeat the same mistakes with the next agency. You want to repeat what actually worked.

What to Do BEFORE Hiring the Next Agency

This is the most important section. Because if you don't change what you do before hiring the next agency, you'll end up firing them for the same reasons. According to Harvard Business Review, the average cost of a bad marketing hire or vendor relationship is significantly higher than the direct fee, with productivity loss, ramp time, and course correction typically 3-5x the direct cost.

Build Your Data Baseline First

Before you hand another agency a budget, you need to know:

  • Your real CAC (customer acquisition cost, calculated like we did above)
  • Your real LTV (lifetime value, accounting for all costs)
  • Your real contribution margin (what's left after all direct and indirect costs)
  • The breakdown by channel (if you have multiple channels, what's the unit economics for each)

You should be able to answer this question in 30 seconds: "What's our target CAC and what's our LTV?" If you can't, don't hire an agency yet.

Define What Success Looks Like in Numbers, Not Vibes

Not: "grow our business." Not: "improve performance." Not: "help us scale."

Write down the actual numbers:

  • "We want to acquire new customers for no more than $X per customer"
  • "We want to grow revenue by X% per month"
  • "We want to maintain an ROAS of at least X, after accounting for all costs"
  • "We want to reduce CAC by X% while maintaining our current LTV"

Write these down. Put them in the contract. Reference them in every conversation. This is what you're hiring the agency to hit. Not to try. To hit.

Require Any New Agency to Report Against Your Numbers

Not their numbers. Your numbers.

In the contract, require monthly reporting that includes:

  • Spend (from their platform)
  • Raw conversions (from their platform)
  • Your actual customers acquired (from your source of truth)
  • Your real CAC (spend ÷ your actual new customers)
  • How much they moved the needle on your target KPI

If the agency can't report this way, they're the wrong agency. They're not set up to be accountable to your business. They're set up to report to their dashboard.

Ask Every Agency: How Do You Reconcile Platform Reporting to Actual Revenue?

This is the interview question that separates good agencies from the rest.

Ask: "How do you reconcile your platform-reported conversions to our actual business revenue? Walk me through your process."

A good agency will say something like: "We export your raw campaign data. We pull your customer data from Shopify. We match them up. We calculate your real CAC. We report against that monthly. And we optimize to hit your CAC targets, not the platform's conversion targets."

If they pause, or say "well, it depends," or talk about how "attribution is complex," or give you a vague answer, it's the wrong agency. It's the same agency you've hired four times already.

Get a Data Audit Done First

Before you hand a new agency a budget, get someone to audit your data. Do you have a clear connection between your ad platforms and your actual revenue? Do your systems talk to each other? Do your numbers reconcile?

If they don't, fix that first. An agency can't perform miracles with a broken data foundation. And you can't evaluate them without one.

This might take 2-4 weeks. It's worth it. Because it's the difference between hiring Agency #6 and having the same outcome, or hiring Agency #6 and actually having the data you need to see what they're delivering.

The Core Issue: You Can't Evaluate Vendors Without Ground Truth

Let me bring this back to the core insight that should drive everything you do.

You can't fire your agency with confidence until you have ground truth. And ground truth means your actual revenue, reconciled to your ad spend, so you can see what's actually being delivered.

Right now, you're in a situation where:

  • Your agency controls the reporting
  • You see dashboards that show good numbers
  • But your actual revenue hasn't moved
  • You don't know why
  • So you fire them
  • And hire someone else to fail in the same way

The reason the cycle repeats is because nothing has changed. You still don't have independent visibility. You're still trusting platform dashboards. You're still unable to verify.

The way you break the cycle is simple: build the data foundation that lets you see the truth. Not the agency's truth. Your truth. Your actual revenue. Your actual customers. Your actual CAC. Your actual LTV.

Once you have that, you can evaluate any agency objectively. And if they fail to hit the number you defined, you can fire them with confidence knowing the number is real, not a feeling.

That's the difference between firing an agency you're frustrated with and firing an agency because the math doesn't work. One is emotional. The other is factual. Be the founder who makes factual decisions.
