The Pre-Growth-Hire Checklist: 17 Things to Audit Before You Hire for Growth

By Tyler Sass | Published February 25, 2026 | Updated February 26, 2026 | 22 min read

The most expensive marketing hire is the one you make before you know what is broken. I have watched dozens of founder-led businesses between $3M and $10M follow the same pattern: revenue stalls, the founder hires a marketing director, and six months later the director has optimized campaigns, rebuilt email funnels, and rewritten positioning. But revenue did not accelerate. The hire did not fail. The system underneath them did. This checklist exists so you never make that mistake.
$300K+
Average cost of a failed marketing leadership hire (salary + ramp + opportunity cost + severance)
Source: Heidrick & Struggles Executive Hiring Benchmark, 2024

That number includes six months of salary ($75K to $100K for a director level hire), benefits, the tools and budget they burn through while diagnosing problems you should have already mapped, and the opportunity cost of 6 to 12 months of stalled growth. It does not include the cost of doing it again when the first hire does not work out.

This is not a theoretical problem. In one engagement, I found that a $9M industrial distribution company was reporting a blended CAC of $181 per customer. Their actual fully loaded blended CAC was $2,800. They had hired two marketing directors in three years. Both "failed." Neither actually failed. They were both given a system that could not produce the results the founder expected because the foundation was broken.

I built this checklist from the patterns I have seen across 40+ revenue audits. Every item represents something that, when missing, causes a growth hire to spend their first 3 to 6 months on infrastructure instead of growth. That is $50K to $100K in salary you are paying someone to do work that should have been done before they started.

How to Use This Checklist

Score yourself honestly on each of the 17 items below. If 5 or more are incomplete, fix operations first. A great marketing hire given a broken system produces broken results faster. You will have a more expensive problem, not a solved one. Skip to the scoring section after you complete the audit.

Why Growth Hires Fail (It Is Usually Not the Hire)

Here is the pattern I see repeated across founder-led businesses between $2M and $10M in revenue.

Revenue growth stalls. The founder looks at the team and thinks, "We need more firepower in marketing." They write a job description, interview a dozen candidates, pick someone experienced, give them access to ad platforms, email, analytics, and the CRM. Then they wait.

Six months in, the new director has optimized campaigns, rebuilt the email funnel, rewritten positioning, and launched new creative. The founder checks the metrics. Conversion is slightly better. CAC is slightly lower (on paper). But revenue did not accelerate. Growth did not compound. The needle barely moved.

The founder fires the director and hires someone else. The cycle repeats.

67%
Of marketing leadership hires at companies under $15M fail within 18 months
Source: Pavilion Revenue Collective Research, 2024

That failure rate is not because two thirds of marketing directors are incompetent. It is because two thirds of the systems they are dropped into are not capable of producing the results the founder expects. The hire did not fail. The foundation did.

I have seen this firsthand. When I audited Prime USA Scales, they had already gone through two marketing directors in three years. Both were solid operators. The problem was that the reported CAC was off by 15x, the CRM data was unreliable, there was no documented sales process, and nobody knew which customers were actually profitable. No marketing director on earth could have succeeded in that environment.

The solution is not to hire better. The solution is to fix the foundation so that a good hire can actually do their job.

That is what this checklist maps.

Key Takeaway

Growth hires fail because of broken systems, not broken people. Two thirds of marketing leadership hires at companies under $15M fail within 18 months. The checklist below identifies the 17 infrastructure gaps that cause those failures. Fix the system first. Then the hire works.

Section 1: Financial Foundation

These five items define whether you know the financial reality of your business well enough to evaluate a growth hire's performance. Without them, every conversation about budget, ROI, and performance becomes guesswork.

1. You know your actual blended CAC (not platform reported ROAS)

Start here. Blended CAC is the real cost of acquiring a new customer when you add up all channels, all time, all costs. Most founders know their Facebook ROAS. They know their Google ROAS. Then they add the two numbers together and call it "blended." That is not blended. That is platform math.

Real blended CAC includes paid media spend, content production costs, email platform fees, sales salaries (pro-rated for time spent on acquisition), tools and software, fulfillment costs for first orders, payment processing fees, and everything else that touched that customer before they became a customer.

When I ran the first 10 revenue audits for Logic Based Marketing, the blended CAC was wrong in every single one by an average of 3.2x. One company thought their CAC was $181. It was $2,800. That is not a rounding error. That is a completely different business reality.

Cost of skipping: Your growth hire's success will be measured against CAC. If the baseline number is wrong by 3x to 15x, you will never know if they are helping or hurting. You might fire someone who actually saved you money. Or keep someone who is driving you broke. Every budget decision they make will be based on a number that does not reflect reality.
How to fix it: Pull your total spend on everything related to acquisition for the last 12 months. Divide by new customers acquired. That is your starting point. Our fully loaded blended CAC guide walks through the complete calculation, including the 7 cost categories most businesses miss.

2. You know who your best customers actually are (from data, not intuition)

This is not about having a polished ICP document with buyer personas and stock photos. It is about looking at your actual customer data and answering three questions: Which customers stay the longest? Which customers spend the most over their lifetime? Which customers cause the fewest support tickets, refunds, and operational headaches?

The intersection of those three answers is your real ICP. Most businesses have never calculated it. They rely on intuition, which is often wrong. I have seen e-commerce businesses optimize their entire acquisition strategy for a customer segment that had the highest volume but the worst retention and lowest margins.

Cost of skipping: Without this data, any marketer you hire will optimize for volume instead of fit. They will fill the pipeline with customers who churn faster, cost more to service, and produce lower margins. Your next hire inherits a worse problem and gets blamed for it.
How to fix it: Pull your last 100 customers. Calculate retention (months active), lifetime revenue, and support burden for each. Segment by whatever makes sense for your business (industry, company size, product line, acquisition channel). The segment with the highest retention and highest LTV is your actual ICP. Write it down.

3. You have an LTV calculation and know your LTV:CAC ratio

LTV is the lifetime value of a customer. Not annual value. Lifetime. It is the total gross profit (not revenue, profit) you expect from that customer over the entire time they remain a customer.

The LTV:CAC ratio is the single most important metric in your business. Below 2:1 and you are buying customers you cannot afford. At 3:1 to 5:1 you have a healthy, scalable business. Above 5:1 you are probably underinvesting in growth.

Most founder-led businesses I audit have never calculated LTV at all. They know their average order value. They sometimes know their average number of orders per customer. But they have not calculated the actual profit per customer over the full lifecycle, factoring in churn, refunds, support costs, and discount rates.

Cost of skipping: Growth without LTV tracking is growth without a sustainability check. Your new hire needs this number to make every budget allocation decision. Without it, every conversation about spend becomes "Can we afford this?" instead of "Does this math work?" Those are fundamentally different questions. See our revenue operations statistics for benchmarks.
How to fix it: Calculate average gross profit per customer per year. Multiply by average customer lifespan in years. That is a basic LTV. Divide by your blended CAC from item 1. If the result is below 3, you have work to do before you hire.

4. You have identified your most profitable customer segment

Revenue and profit are different. Your biggest customer might be your lowest margin customer. Your smallest customer segment might be your most profitable one.

I see this constantly with e-commerce businesses. They have one product line that drives 60% of revenue but only 15% of profit. And another product line that drives 10% of revenue but 40% of profit. The marketing director, if they do not know this, will optimize for the 60% revenue line. Margins will erode. The founder will be confused about why "sales are up but profit is down."

Cost of skipping: Without margin data by segment, your new hire will optimize for customers that look good on revenue reports but destroy margin. You will scale a business that is unprofitable. This is one of the 23 signs your revenue operations are broken.
How to fix it: Pull your last 50 to 100 customers. Calculate gross margin (not just revenue) for each one. Group by segment. The segment with the highest average margin and best retention is where your marketing should focus. Give this data to your new hire on day one.

5. You know what "a good month" looks like in measurable terms

This is the performance bar. What does success actually look like? Is it $200K in new MRR? Is it 50 qualified leads? Is it a certain LTV:CAC ratio? A specific churn rate?

You need a specific, measurable definition of a good month that is documented and agreed upon before your new hire starts. Not "we will know it when we see it." Actual numbers.

Without this, you will be in a perpetual argument about whether the hire is working. Some months you will say "this is good" and other months you will say "this is bad" when the metrics are identical. The new hire will not know if they are succeeding or failing because the goalposts move based on how the founder feels that week.

Cost of skipping: Performance management becomes impossible. You will evaluate the hire based on your mood instead of data. You will fire good people and keep bad ones because you have no standard. The cost is not just the wasted salary. It is the signal it sends to your team that success is undefined and arbitrary.
How to fix it: Write down 3 to 5 KPIs that define a good month. Set a specific number for each. Share them with your new hire before their first day. Review them monthly. Adjust quarterly if needed, but never mid-month.
Key Takeaway: Financial Foundation

If you cannot answer "What does a customer actually cost us?" and "What is a customer actually worth?" then you are not ready to hire someone to get you more of them. These five items are the minimum financial literacy required to evaluate a growth hire's performance. Without them, you are guessing.

Section 2: Data Infrastructure

Your growth hire will need data to make decisions. These four items determine whether that data exists, whether it is accurate, and whether they can trust it enough to act on it.

6. GA4 is installed AND configured with goals and event tracking

Installed and configured are two very different things. "Installed" means the GA4 code is on your website. "Configured" means you have set up events for every meaningful user action: form submissions, product views, add-to-cart actions, checkout completions, email signups, demo requests, and conversion events.

Most businesses have GA4 installed. Most have not configured it past the defaults. Your new marketing hire will ask for data on page performance, funnel conversion rates, and traffic quality by channel. If the events are not set up, they will get blank screens where data should be. They will spend their first month setting up tracking instead of driving acquisition.

Cost of skipping: One month of a marketing director's salary ($10K to $15K) spent configuring analytics instead of acquiring customers. Plus the decision-quality cost of every week they operate without data. That is easily $20K to $30K in combined direct cost and opportunity cost.
How to fix it: Open GA4. Go to Events. If you only see default events (page_view, session_start, first_visit), you need to configure custom events. Set up a conversion event for every action that represents revenue or pipeline: form submit, purchase, demo booked, email captured. This takes a few hours, not weeks. Do it before the hire starts.

7. You have attribution across channels (or you know exactly where it is broken)

Attribution answers the question: "Which channel gets credit for this customer?" Facebook thinks it deserves credit. Google thinks it deserves credit. Your email probably touched the customer too. Your organic content might have been the first touchpoint. Who actually drove the conversion?

Perfect attribution does not exist. Every model has blind spots. But there is a massive difference between "we have a documented attribution approach with known limitations" and "we have no idea which channels are working." The first is workable. The second is a black hole.

Our marketing attribution guide covers the common models and their tradeoffs in detail. At minimum, your new hire needs to know which model you are using, what it captures, and what it misses.

Cost of skipping: The first thing a good marketing hire will do is audit attribution. If they find a mess with no documentation, they will spend 2 to 3 months investigating and rebuilding the infrastructure before they can make any channel allocation decisions. If you have already documented the gaps, they can work within the constraints instead of mapping them from scratch.
How to fix it: Write a one-page document that answers: (1) What attribution model are we using? (2) Which channels are tracked? (3) Where do we know tracking is broken or incomplete? (4) What is the gap between platform-reported conversions and actual revenue? That document saves your new hire weeks.

8. You know which channels produce your best customers (not most customers)

Volume and quality are different things. You might get 100 signups from Facebook and 20 from organic search. But if those 20 organic customers stay three times longer, spend twice as much, and generate zero support tickets, organic search is the better channel by every metric that matters.

Most businesses optimize for volume. They look at cost per lead and choose the cheapest channel. That is how you fill your pipeline with low-quality customers who churn fast, demand refunds, and destroy your margins.

Cost of skipping: Without channel quality data, your new hire will allocate budget based on cost-per-lead. The cheapest channel will get the most budget. The cheapest channel almost always produces the lowest-quality customers. You will scale volume while degrading margin. By the time you notice, you have spent 6 months and $50K+ on the wrong strategy.
How to fix it: Pull your last 100 customers. Tag each one with the primary acquisition channel. Calculate LTV and retention rate by channel. Rank channels by customer quality, not volume. Share this with your new hire on day one.

9. Your CRM data is accurate (deals, contacts, and pipeline reflect reality)

Open your CRM right now. Pull up 20 random deals. Are they in the right stage? Are the close dates realistic? Does the pipeline total match what you actually expect to close this quarter?

In most $3M to $10M businesses I audit, the answer is no. Deals sit in stages for months without updates. Close dates are aspirational rather than realistic. Contact information is incomplete. The pipeline total bears no resemblance to what will actually close.

Your marketing hire will use the CRM to forecast, to plan campaigns, to measure conversion rates at every stage, and to calculate cost per opportunity. If the data is wrong, every forecast will be wrong, every conversion rate will be misleading, and every budget decision will be based on fiction.

Cost of skipping: Pipeline forecasting becomes impossible. Your hire will produce reports that look professional but contain garbage data. You will make strategic decisions based on those reports. When the forecast misses by 40%, the hire gets blamed for a data quality problem that existed before they arrived. The real cost is the bad decisions you make based on bad data.
How to fix it: Dedicate one week to a CRM cleanup. Go through every active deal. Update stages. Remove dead deals. Correct close dates. Verify contact information. Set a weekly 15-minute CRM hygiene habit. This is the single highest-leverage operational improvement most businesses can make.
Key Takeaway: Data Infrastructure

A growth hire without clean data is a pilot without instruments. They can still fly the plane, but they are guessing about altitude, speed, and heading. The cost of that guessing is measured in wasted budget, wrong channel bets, and strategic decisions built on fiction. Fix the data layer before you hand someone the controls.

Section 3: Sales Process and Pipeline

Marketing does not exist in a vacuum. It feeds into sales. If the sales process is undefined, undocumented, or broken, even the best marketing will produce leads that die in the pipeline. These four items ensure the handoff from marketing to revenue actually works.

10. Your sales process is documented (stages, definitions, handoff criteria)

Can you answer this question: "What is a lead? What makes a lead become an opportunity? What makes an opportunity become a deal? What defines each stage?"

If your answer is "my sales person knows," you have failed this item.

The sales process does not need to be complex. It needs to be written down. One page. Five stages. Clear definitions of what moves someone from one stage to the next. Time expectations for each stage. Who owns each stage.

Without this, every new hire reinvents the sales motion. The marketing hire designs campaigns targeting one customer journey while the sales team is running a completely different one. Leads fall through cracks. Handoff points are undefined. Nobody knows whose fault it is when deals stall.

Cost of skipping: Measurement becomes impossible. If stages are not defined, you cannot measure conversion rates between stages. If you cannot measure conversion rates, you cannot identify where the funnel breaks. If you cannot identify where the funnel breaks, your marketing hire is optimizing blind. This is one of the clearest signs of broken revenue operations.
How to fix it: Write a one-page sales process document. List each stage. Define what qualifies a contact to enter that stage. Define what moves them to the next stage. Define who owns each transition. Share it with sales and marketing before day one.

11. You can explain why your last 5 deals were won or lost (with specific reasons)

"Price" is not a reason. "Timing" is not a reason. Those are symptoms. Go deeper.

"We lost the deal because the economic buyer was the CFO and our pitch focused on marketing ROI without addressing cash flow impact." That is a reason. It is specific. It is testable. It tells your marketing hire exactly what messaging angle to address.

"We won because the prospect had just fired their agency and needed someone who could prove measurable results within 90 days, which matched our diagnostic timeline." That is a reason. It tells your marketing hire which pain points to target and which timing signals to watch for.

Win/loss data is the raw material for positioning. A marketing director without it is writing messaging in a vacuum and hoping it lands.

Cost of skipping: Your hire will write messaging based on assumptions. They will spend 2 to 3 months testing copy that misses the mark because it was never informed by actual buyer behavior. At $10K to $15K per month in salary plus ad spend, that is $30K to $60K testing messaging that your existing win/loss data could have informed from day one.
How to fix it: Call or email your last 5 won customers and your last 5 lost prospects. Ask two questions: "What was the deciding factor in your decision?" and "What almost made you choose differently?" Write down the answers. Those 10 data points are more valuable than any market research report.

12. You have at least one working email automation beyond a welcome sequence

Email is the most underutilized growth channel in most founder-led businesses. Most companies have a welcome sequence. Then people sit idle. No nurture. No re-engagement. No win-back. No product education. No upsell. Just welcome and silence.

A single working automation beyond welcome proves two things: (1) you have the infrastructure to run automated campaigns, and (2) you have the content and segmentation to power them. Both are prerequisites for a growth hire to build on.

If your new hire starts from zero on email, they will spend 2 to 3 months building sequences, writing copy, setting up integrations, and testing deliverability. That is infrastructure work, not growth work.

Cost of skipping: Email is almost always the fastest ROI channel. Industry data shows email generates $36 to $42 in revenue for every $1 spent. If your email infrastructure is at zero, your new hire's fastest revenue lever is locked behind months of setup work. Every month that email is not working is a month of missed revenue.
How to fix it: Build one automated sequence beyond welcome. A 4 to 6 email nurture sequence for leads who did not convert is the highest-value starting point. Use whatever platform you already have (Mailchimp, ActiveCampaign, HubSpot). Imperfect and running beats perfect and planned.

13. You have a retention and expansion tracking metric

What percentage of customers are you keeping? What is your monthly churn rate? Your annual churn? Do you track NPS, CSAT, or any equivalent measure of customer health?

If the answer is "I do not know," pick a number today. That becomes the baseline. It does not need to be precise. It needs to exist. Because great marketing should improve retention, not just drive acquisition. And your new hire should be evaluated on customer quality and retention, not just new logos.

A business that acquires 50 customers per month but loses 45 has a retention problem, not an acquisition problem. Hiring a marketing director to acquire more customers for a business with 90% churn is pouring water into a bucket with a hole in the bottom.

Cost of skipping: Without retention data, your new hire has no feedback loop on customer quality. They will optimize for acquisition volume because that is what they can measure. Customer quality will degrade. Churn will increase. You will blame the marketing hire for "low quality leads" when the real problem is that nobody measured quality from the beginning.
How to fix it: Pick one retention metric. Monthly churn rate is the easiest starting point. Calculate it for the last 6 months. Set a target. Add it to the KPIs your new hire will be measured against. Now marketing has a quality signal, not just a volume signal.
Key Takeaway: Sales Process and Pipeline

Marketing generates leads. Sales converts them. If the handoff is broken, the leads die. A documented sales process, win/loss data, working email automation, and retention tracking are the four elements that ensure marketing's output connects to revenue. Without them, your growth hire is generating leads into a void.

Section 4: Strategic Clarity

These final four items determine whether your new hire has the strategic context to make good decisions. Without them, they will build strategy from scratch, which means 3 to 6 months of learning things you already know (or should know) before any real growth work begins.

14. Your offer is clear and differentiated (one sentence explains why you, not what you do)

"We help companies find revenue leaks" is what you do. "We find the $50K to $200K of lost revenue hiding in your current funnel in 30 days" is why you matter. The difference is everything.

Your marketing hire amplifies and distributes positioning. They do not invent it from scratch. If your positioning does not exist yet, define it before the hire starts. If it exists but is unclear, sharpen it.

When positioning is undefined, the marketing director spends weeks interviewing the team, analyzing competitors, testing messages, and trying to infer what makes the business unique. That is strategy work the founder should own. Not because the director cannot do it, but because only the founder has the context to get it right quickly.

Cost of skipping: The marketing director will create positioning based on inference and competitor analysis. It will be competent but generic. The messaging will sound like everyone else in your space because it was not informed by the founder's unique insight and proof points. Generic positioning produces generic results.
How to fix it: Write one sentence that answers: "Why should a customer choose us over doing nothing?" (Not over competitors. Over inaction. That is the real alternative.) Test it with 5 customers. If they nod, you have it. If they look confused, keep iterating.

15. You have a documented customer acquisition journey (even rough)

Map it out. Where do potential customers first learn about you? (Awareness.) What information do they need to evaluate you? (Consideration.) What reassures them enough to buy? (Decision.) What happens after they buy? (Onboarding.)

It does not need to be fancy. It does not need a software tool. A whiteboard sketch that answers those four questions is sufficient. The point is that it exists and it is coherent.

Without this map, your marketing hire will optimize individual channels without seeing the full picture. They will pour budget into awareness while the consideration stage leaks. Or they will optimize the bottom of the funnel while the top produces the wrong audience. Understanding how revenue operations work in small businesses requires seeing the entire journey, not just one piece.

Cost of skipping: Budget goes to the wrong stage. Your hire optimizes what they can see (usually paid ads) while ignoring the stages they cannot see (usually the handoff from marketing to sales, or from first purchase to repeat purchase). The funnel "improves" in one area while getting worse overall.
How to fix it: Draw four boxes: Awareness, Consideration, Decision, Onboarding. Under each one, write what currently happens. Identify the biggest gap. That is where your new hire should focus first. Give them this map on day one.

16. You have a diagnostic that tells you which part of the funnel is breaking

"Conversion is low" is not actionable. "Conversion from lead to opportunity is 4% against a target of 12%" is actionable. The difference is the diagnostic layer.

A diagnostic could be as simple as a monthly review of conversion rates by stage. How many visitors become leads? How many leads become opportunities? How many opportunities become customers? When you see those numbers, the bottleneck becomes obvious.

Without a diagnostic, your new hire will spend their first 2 to 3 months building the measurement infrastructure before they can identify what to fix. That is diagnostic work, not growth work. Businesses that have hit the $10M wall almost always have this diagnostic blind spot.

Cost of skipping: Your hire spends months diagnosing instead of fixing. They build dashboards and measurement systems while the actual revenue problems go unsolved. You are paying growth-hire salary for analytics-hire work. The opportunity cost of 3 months of delayed growth in a $5M business is $100K to $250K in unrealized revenue.
How to fix it: Create a simple funnel report. List each stage. Count the people in each stage last month. Calculate the conversion rate between stages. Identify the stage with the lowest conversion rate. That is your bottleneck. Your new hire should see this report on day one.

17. You have a 12-month view of revenue by channel, segment, and product line

Historical data. By channel. By segment. By product. The last 12 months, month by month. This is the baseline every marketing plan is built from.

Your new hire will look at this and see patterns you cannot see because you are too close to the business. "Channel A is declining 8% month over month, I need to investigate why." "Segment B has high variance, which offers are working for that segment?" "Product line C has the highest margins but the lowest volume, should we shift budget?"

Without this data, they are setting goals without a starting point. They will make confident projections based on assumptions and get blamed when those projections miss by 30% to 50%.

Cost of skipping: Every marketing plan your new hire creates will be built on assumptions instead of data. Budget allocation will be based on industry benchmarks (which may not apply to your business) instead of your actual historical performance. Strategic direction will be generic instead of tailored. The cost is not just inaccuracy. It is the compounding effect of wrong decisions over 6 to 12 months.
How to fix it: Export your revenue data from your accounting software or CRM. Break it out by month, by channel (if possible), by product line, and by customer segment. Put it in a spreadsheet. It does not need to be pretty. It needs to exist. This exercise alone will show you patterns you have been missing.
Key Takeaway: Strategic Clarity

Strategy is not the marketing director's job to invent. It is the founder's job to define and the director's job to execute and refine. Clear positioning, a mapped customer journey, a diagnostic framework, and historical revenue data give your new hire the strategic context they need to make smart decisions from day one instead of spending months learning what you already know.

How to Score Yourself

Count how many of the 17 items you can honestly check off. Not "we sort of have this." Not "someone on the team probably knows." Actually documented, accessible, and accurate.

5+
Number of missing items that should stop you from hiring. Fix operations first.
Source: Logic Based Marketing analysis of 40+ revenue audits
17/17 Items Complete

You are ready to hire. Your system is built. A great marketing leader can walk in and grow a functioning machine. Your job is to define the targets (revenue, CAC, LTV, churn) and get out of the way. This is the ideal state. It is also rare. Fewer than 10% of the businesses I audit are here.

12 to 16 Items Complete

You are close. Fix the remaining gaps before your new hire's first day, not after. Each missing item represents 1 to 3 weeks of time your hire will spend on infrastructure instead of growth. Close the gaps in 2 to 4 weeks. Then hire. Do not hire and fix in parallel. That never works because the hire will build on top of the gaps and you will have to rip it out later.

7 to 11 Items Complete

Not yet. You have infrastructure problems that a great hire will spend their first 6 months solving at your expense. A marketing director at $120K to $180K annual salary spending 6 months on infrastructure is $60K to $90K in wasted growth-hire budget. Hire a fractional operations person or consultant to close the gaps first. Then bring on the growth hire when the foundation is ready.

Below 7 Items Complete

Stop. Do not hire. The hire will fail for reasons completely unrelated to their ability. You do not have an acquisition problem. You have a foundation problem. Take 60 to 90 days and build the operations layer. The investment will pay for itself because when you do hire, they will be effective from month one instead of month seven.

What to Fix First (By Score Range)

If you scored below 17, here is the priority order for closing gaps. Fix in this sequence because each item builds on the one before it.

If You Scored 12 to 16: Quick Fixes (2 to 4 Weeks)

Priority Item Time to Fix Why It Is First
1 Blended CAC calculation (#1) 2 to 3 days Everything else references this number
2 CRM data cleanup (#9) 1 week The pipeline must reflect reality before anyone can plan
3 GA4 configuration (#6) 2 to 3 days Data needs to be flowing before the hire starts measuring
4 Success metrics (#5) 1 day The hire needs to know what they are aiming for

If You Scored 7 to 11: Foundation Work (60 to 90 Days)

Phase Items Duration Output
Phase 1 Financial Foundation (#1 through #5) Weeks 1 to 3 Know your CAC, LTV, margins, and targets
Phase 2 Data Infrastructure (#6 through #9) Weeks 3 to 6 Clean CRM, configured analytics, attribution documented

If You Scored Below 7: Full Reset (90+ Days)

You need a revenue operations foundation before you hire anyone for growth. This is not a criticism. It is a diagnosis. The businesses I have seen recover fastest from this state are the ones that invest in a systematic audit first.

The sequence is the same (financial, data, sales, strategy) but the work is deeper because most of these items need to be built from scratch rather than refined. Consider hiring a fractional operations consultant or running a revenue diagnostic to identify which gaps are causing the most damage.

Key Takeaway: Prioritization

Fix financial foundation first (especially blended CAC). Then data infrastructure. Then sales process. Then strategic clarity. Each layer depends on the one below it. Trying to fix them out of order wastes time because you end up redoing work when you realize the underlying layer was wrong.

The Real Cost of Hiring Into a Broken System

Let me make this concrete. Here is what hiring a marketing director into a business that scores below 12 on this checklist actually costs.

$161K/mo
Recoverable losses found at one $9M business that had "failed" two marketing directors
Source: Logic Based Marketing, Prime USA Scales engagement

Direct salary cost: A marketing director at a $5M to $10M business commands $120K to $180K annual salary plus benefits. That is $10K to $15K per month fully loaded.

Ramp time cost: In a well-functioning system, a marketing director is productive in 30 to 60 days. In a broken system, they spend 3 to 6 months on infrastructure. That is $30K to $90K in salary before they do any actual growth work.

Opportunity cost: Every month your marketing director spends on infrastructure is a month they are not driving growth. For a $5M business targeting 30% annual growth, each month of delay costs approximately $125K in unrealized revenue.

Tool and budget cost: Marketing directors need budget to operate. Expect $3K to $10K per month in tools, ad spend, and production costs during the ramp period. In a broken system, much of this is spent on diagnostics and testing, not growth.

Replacement cost: If the hire fails (67% probability in a broken system), the replacement cycle costs another $30K to $50K in recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity during the gap.

$300K to $500K
Total cost of one failed growth hire (salary + ramp + opportunity cost + tools + replacement)
