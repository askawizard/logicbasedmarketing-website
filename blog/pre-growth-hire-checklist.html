The Pre-Growth-Hire Checklist: 17 Things to Audit Before You Add to Your Marketing Team

Tyler Sass Feb 25, 2026 11 min read

The most expensive marketing hire is the one made before you know what's broken.

Here's the pattern I've seen repeated across dozens of founder-led businesses: Revenue growth stalls somewhere around $2M to $5M. The founder takes a hard look at the team and thinks, "We need more firepower in marketing." They hire a director or CMO, give them access to ad platforms, email, and analytics, and wait.

Six months in, the director has optimized campaigns, rebuilt the email funnel, and written new positioning. The founder checks the metrics. Conversion is slightly better. CAC is slightly lower. But revenue didn't accelerate. The hire didn't fail—the system did.

Rule of thumb: If 5 or more items on this list are incomplete, fix operations first. A great marketing hire given a broken system produces broken results faster. You will have a more expensive problem, not a solved one.

This checklist isn't about whether you're ready to hire someone. It's about whether the system you'll give them is capable of producing the results you expect.

The 17-Point Pre-Hire Audit

1.
You know your actual blended CAC (not platform-reported ROAS)

Start here. Blended CAC is the real cost of a new customer when you add up all channels, all time, all costs. Most founders know their Facebook ROAS. They know their Google ROAS. Then they add the two numbers together and call it blended. That is not blended. That is platform math.

Real blended CAC includes paid media, content, email production, sales salaries, tools, and everything else that touched that customer before they signed. When I ran the first 10 audits, this number was wrong in every single one by an average of 3.2x.

Why it matters: Any growth hire's success will be measured against CAC. If the baseline number is wrong, you will never know if they are helping or hurting. You might fire someone who actually saved you money, or keep someone who is driving you broke.
2.
You know who your best customers actually are (not just who you think they are)

This isn't about having a formal "ICP document" before you start. It's about looking at the data: which customers stay longest, spend the most, and cause the fewest problems? That pattern tells you who to target next.

Most businesses don't have this defined — and that's fine. It's something that gets built from your actual customer data, not guessed from a whiteboard session. A marketing director without this clarity will optimize for volume. You'll get more leads and higher churn.

Why it matters: Without knowing who your best-fit customers actually are, any marketer you hire will optimize for volume instead of fit. That fills the pipeline with customers who churn faster. Your next hire inherits the problem and gets blamed for it. This is something a Revenue Leak Diagnostic can help define from your existing data.
3.
You have a defined LTV calculation and know your current LTV:CAC ratio

LTV is the lifetime value of a customer. Not annual. Lifetime. The entire profit (not revenue, profit) you expect to make from that customer over the time they stay a customer. That number minus CAC should be at least 3:1 for a healthy business. Less than 2:1 and you are buying customers you cannot afford.

If you do not know this number, your growth hire will spend the first two months arguing about budget allocation with no foundation. If you do know it, every decision becomes obvious.

Why it matters: Growth without LTV tracking is growth without a sustainability check. Your new hire needs this number to operate from. It is the difference between "make more customers" and "make more profitable customers."
4.
You have GA4 installed and configured (not just installed, configured with goals/events tracking)

GA4 is the foundation layer. Installed means the code is there. Configured means the data is flowing correctly. You should have events set up for every meaningful action: form submissions, product trials, email signups, core product usage, and conversion events.

Most businesses have GA4 installed. Most have not configured it past the defaults. Your new marketing hire will ask for data on page performance. If the events are not set up, they will be flying blind.

Why it matters: This is the data foundation. Without it, every reporting conversation will be guesswork. Your hire will spend the first month setting up tracking instead of driving acquisition.
5.
You have attribution across all active channels or you know where your attribution is broken

Attribution is the question: "Which channel gets credit for this customer?" Facebook thinks it deserves credit. Google thinks it deserves credit. Your email probably touched the customer too. Who gets the conversion assigned?

Perfect attribution does not exist. But broken attribution is worse than no attribution because you make confident decisions on bad data. At minimum, you should know that your attribution model is incomplete. Better yet, you have a documented approach.

Why it matters: The first thing a good marketing hire will do is audit attribution. If they find a mess, they will spend their first 3 months fixing infrastructure instead of driving growth. If you have already documented the mess, they can work within the constraints instead of investigating them.
6.
You know which channels have produced your best customers (not most customers, best customers by LTV and fit)

Volume and quality are different. You might get 100 signups from one channel and 20 from another. But if those 20 customers stay three times longer and spend two times more, the channel that produced 20 is the better channel. Most businesses optimize for 100.

Pull your last 100 customers. Look at the channels they came from. Calculate LTV and retention by channel. The answer will change how your new hire allocates budget.

Why it matters: Channel allocation decisions require this data. Without it, your new hire will make gut calls and optimize for the wrong metrics. They will get blamed when quality degrades and volume goes up.
7.
Your CRM has accurate data in it (deals, contacts, and pipeline represent reality)

The CRM is the source of truth for pipeline. If you cannot open your CRM and see the actual state of your business, your marketing hire cannot do their job. They will forecast growth, and the sales team will not be able to hit the forecast because the pipeline data was wrong.

Audit this one thoroughly. Check 20 random deals. Are they in the right stages? Are they realistic? Does the pipeline total match what you actually expect to close?

Why it matters: Pipeline forecasting is impossible with dirty CRM data. You will get inaccurate reporting from day one if this is not cleaned up first. Your hire will be blamed for forecasting errors that started before they arrived.
8.
You have at least one working email automation beyond a welcome sequence

Email is the most underutilized growth channel in most founder-led businesses. Most companies have a welcome sequence. Then people sit idle. No nurture. No re-engagement. No win-back. Just welcome and silence.

This could be a product update sequence, a feature adoption flow, or a re-engagement campaign. Just one automated sequence beyond welcome that is actively running and performing.

Why it matters: Email is almost always the fastest ROI channel available to you. If it is not running, your new hire will spend months building what should already exist. A working email foundation lets them optimize instead of build from zero.
9.
Your sales process is documented (what happens from lead to close, who does what, what defines each stage)

Do you have a documented answer to this question: "What is a lead? What makes it move from lead to opportunity? What makes it move from opportunity to close?" If you answer "my sales person knows," you have failed this item.

Your sales process does not need to be complex. It needs to be documented. One page, five stages, clear definitions of what moves someone from one stage to the next.

Why it matters: Without a documented process, every new hire reinvents the sales motion. This creates measurement problems and slows ramp time. Your marketing hire will struggle to design campaigns if the customer journey is undefined.
10.
You can explain why your last 5 deals were won or lost (with specific reasons, not "price" or "timing")

Price is a symptom, not a reason. Timing is a symptom, not a reason. Go deeper. "They chose our competitor because the integration with their existing workflow was better." "We lost that one because the economic buyer was a finance person and we did not address cash flow impact." Specific. Testable. Repeatable.

If you cannot explain why, you do not have the positioning data your marketing hire needs to succeed. They will write messaging in a vacuum and hope it lands.

Why it matters: Win loss data is the input for positioning decisions. A marketing director without this data writes messaging in a vacuum. They will optimize messaging for the wrong problem and waste budget.
11.
You have a retention and expansion tracking metric (churn rate, NPS, or equivalent)

What percentage of customers are you keeping? What is your annual churn? If you do not know, pick a number today. That becomes the baseline. Great marketing should improve retention, not just acquisition.

Your new hire should be evaluated not just on customers acquired but on customer quality and retention. That requires a metric that lives on the dashboard.

Why it matters: Marketing should protect margin, not just drive acquisition. Without retention data, your new hire has no feedback loop on customer quality. They will optimize for acquisition and degrade customer quality in the process.
12.
You know what "a good month" looks like in measurable terms for your business

This is the performance bar. What does success mean? Is it $200K in new MRR? Is it 50 qualified leads? Is it 25% month-over-month growth? You need a specific, measurable target that defines what a good month looks like.

Without this, you will be in a perpetual argument about whether your new hire is working or not. Some months you will say "This is good." Other months you will say "This is bad" even when the metrics are identical. You need a standard.

Why it matters: Performance management requires agreed-upon metrics. If you cannot define good, you cannot evaluate whether the hire is working. You will fire people based on moving targets and keep people based on luck.
13.
Your offer is clear and differentiated (you can explain in one sentence why a customer should choose you over the alternative)

Not what you do. Why you matter. "We help companies find revenue leaks" is what you do. "We find the $50K to $200K of lost revenue that is hiding in your current funnel in 30 days" is why you matter.

This is not the marketing director's job to invent from scratch. They amplify and distribute the positioning. If it does not exist yet, define it before they start.

Why it matters: Positioning is not the marketing director's job to invent from scratch. They amplify and distribute the positioning. If it does not exist yet, define it first. They will spend weeks inferring it from your ad copy instead of weeks amplifying it across channels.
14.
You have a documented customer acquisition journey from awareness to close (even rough)

Map it out. Awareness stage: where do potential customers first learn about you? Consideration stage: what information do they need to evaluate you? Decision stage: what reassures them to buy? It does not need to be fancy. It needs to exist and be coherent.

Without this map, your marketing hire is building a funnel without seeing the full picture. They will optimize one stage while ignoring the others.

Why it matters: Without this map, your marketing hire is building a funnel without seeing the full picture. They will optimize one stage while ignoring the others. Budget will go to the wrong place and the fundamental funnel will not improve.
15.
You have identified your most profitable customer segment (not your highest-revenue customers, your highest-margin customers)

Revenue and profit are different. Your biggest customer might be your lowest-margin customer. Your smallest customer segment might be your most profitable one. Know which one is which.

Pull your last 50 customers. Calculate the gross margin for each one. Find the segment with the highest average margin. That is the segment your marketing should focus on.

Why it matters: Great marketers target the right ICP, not just any ICP. They need this data to focus spend correctly. Without it, they will optimize for customers that look good on revenue reports but destroy margin.
16.
You have a diagnostic or audit mechanism that tells you which part of the funnel is breaking

Not just that conversion is low. But which part of the funnel is the problem? Is awareness working but consideration is failing? Is consideration working but decision is failing? A diagnostic tool could be as simple as a monthly review of conversion rates by stage.

"Conversion is low" is not actionable. "Conversion from lead to opportunity is 4% against a target of 12%" is actionable.

Why it matters: Your new hire needs to know where to focus first. "Conversion is low" is not actionable. "Conversion from MQL to SQL is 4% against a target of 12%" is. They can spend the first week on the right problem instead of diagnosing in the dark.
17.
You have a 12-month view of revenue by channel, customer segment, and product line

Historical revenue data. By channel. By segment. By product. The last 12 months, month by month. This is the baseline every marketing plan is built from.

Your new hire will look at this and say "Channel A is declining, I need to investigate why" or "Segment B has high variance, I need to understand which offers are working." Without this data, they are setting goals out of thin air.

Why it matters: This is the baseline every marketing plan is built from. Without it, your new hire is setting goals without a starting point. They will make confident projections on historical noise and get blamed when those projections miss.

How to Score Yourself

17/17 items

You are ready to hire. A great marketing leader can come in and grow a functioning system. Your job is to define the bar (good months, LTV targets, CAC targets) and they execute against it. This is the ideal state.

12-16 items

Close. Fix the gaps before your new hire's first week, not after. You are missing a few infrastructure pieces. Close them quickly. Do not hire and fix in parallel.

7-11 items

Not yet. You have infrastructure problems that a great hire will spend their first 6 months on (at your expense). Hire later. Build operations now. Your hire's time is too expensive to waste on infrastructure diagnostics.

Below 7 items

Stop. The hire will fail for reasons unrelated to their ability. Fix the foundation. You will get a more expensive version of your current problem. Take three months and build the operations layer.
